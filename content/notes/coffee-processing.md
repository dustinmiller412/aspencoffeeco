<p class="lead">Before coffee ever shows up in your kitchen, it starts life as a fruit. Sticky, sweet, temperamental fruit that has far more influence on your cup than most people realize.</p>

A sticky, sweet, temperamental fruit that will absolutely ruin your day if you leave it on the counter too long.

Most people never think about this. Coffee arrives in a bag, brown and roasted, smelling like the best part of the morning. The fruit feels like a different world entirely. But understanding what happens between the tree and your cup explains almost everything about why coffee tastes the way it does — and why the same bean, grown in the same place, can taste completely different depending on who processed it and how.

## The Part Everyone Skips

<div class="callout callout--grid">
  <div class="callout-col">
    <p class="callout-label">Starts here</p>
    <p class="callout-body">Ripe cherry picked from the tree.</p>
  </div>
  <div class="callout-col">
    <p class="callout-label">Critical step</p>
    <p class="callout-body">How the fruit is removed and how the seed is dried.</p>
  </div>
  <div class="callout-col">
    <p class="callout-label">Why it matters</p>
    <p class="callout-body">It can make two coffees from the same farm taste like completely different lots.</p>
  </div>
</div>

No matter what kind of coffee you're drinking, every single bean went through the same basic journey. Grown on a tree. Picked as a ripe cherry. Stripped of its fruit somehow. Dried. Rested. Milled. Shipped as green coffee. Roasted.

*Processing* is simply what happens during the "strip and dry" part of that journey.

Those decisions — how quickly the fruit is removed, how long the bean sits in its own sugars, how it dries — have an outsized effect on the final cup. Two coffees from the same farm, the same harvest, processed differently, can taste like they came from entirely different countries.

Most of what you'll find falls into three categories: washed, natural, and honey.

---

## Washed Coffee — *Clean, structured, and quietly confident*

<div class="callout">
  <p class="callout-label">In one line</p>
  <p class="callout-body">Fruit comes off early, so the bean itself does most of the talking.</p>
</div>

Washed coffees get their fruit removed almost immediately after harvest. The cherry is pulped, the beans are fermented briefly in water to loosen any remaining sugars, then rinsed clean before drying.

Because the fruit doesn't stick around long, the coffee itself gets to speak — not the sweetness of what surrounded it.

<div class="callout">
  <p class="callout-label">What washed coffees tend to taste like</p>
  <ul class="callout-list">
    <li>Clean and clear, like there's nothing in the way.</li>
    <li>Gently bright, with soft acidity.</li>
    <li>Structured and predictable in a good way.</li>
    <li>Familiar notes — chocolate, caramel, soft citrus.</li>
  </ul>
</div>

These are the coffees that just *work*. Easy to brew, easy to drink, hard to mess up. If you reach for the same bag every morning without overthinking it, there's a good chance it's washed.

<div class="callout callout--warm">
  <p class="callout-label">What to look for</p>
  <p class="callout-body">If you like clarity and brightness, start with <a href="/collections/single-origin">washed single origins</a>. This is usually where origin character shows up most clearly.</p>
</div>

---

## Natural Coffee — *Loud, expressive, and not apologizing for it*

<div class="callout">
  <p class="callout-label">In one line</p>
  <p class="callout-body">The whole fruit dries around the bean, and the cup carries that fruit with it.</p>
</div>

Natural coffees take the opposite approach entirely. Instead of removing the fruit right away, the whole cherry — skin, fruit, and all — is laid out to dry intact. Over weeks, the sugars and flavors from the fruit slowly migrate into the bean.

The result is coffee that carries the fruit with it into your cup.

<div class="callout">
  <p class="callout-label">What natural coffees tend to taste like</p>
  <ul class="callout-list">
    <li>Juicy and fruit-forward — berries, tropical fruit, sometimes stone fruit.</li>
    <li>Heavier in body, sometimes almost syrupy.</li>
    <li>Softer structure, with less of that clean brightness.</li>
    <li>Occasionally a little wild.</li>
  </ul>
</div>

When it's done well, a natural coffee is the thing that makes someone who thought they didn't like coffee say *"wait, coffee can taste like this?"* When it's done poorly, it tastes like a fermentation experiment that lost the plot halfway through.

The margin for error is smaller. The reward when it works is significant.

<div class="callout callout--warm">
  <p class="callout-label">Who it's for</p>
  <p class="callout-body">If you want coffee that reads more like fruit than roast, natural lots are usually the place to start.</p>
</div>

---

## Honey Coffee — *The middle ground that somehow works better than it has any right to*

<div class="callout">
  <p class="callout-label">In one line</p>
  <p class="callout-body">Some fruit stays on during drying, so you get more sweetness than washed with more structure than natural.</p>
</div>

Honey processing lives between the two. The outer skin of the cherry is removed — so it's not fully natural — but some of the sticky fruit layer underneath, called mucilage, is intentionally left on the bean while it dries.

How much mucilage stays on determines the character of the coffee. Less fruit left on means something closer to washed. More fruit means something closer to natural. This is why honey coffees can taste meaningfully different from one another even within the same category.

<div class="callout">
  <p class="callout-label">What honey coffees tend to taste like</p>
  <ul class="callout-list">
    <li>Sweeter than washed, more structured than natural.</li>
    <li>Soft and smooth in body.</li>
    <li>Gently complex without demanding all your attention.</li>
    <li>The kind of coffee people call elegant once they stop pretending they hate that word.</li>
  </ul>
</div>

They occupy a particular kind of space in the lineup — approachable enough for everyday drinking, interesting enough to pay attention to.

<div class="callout callout--warm">
  <p class="callout-label">If you are not sure where to start</p>
  <p class="callout-body">Honey coffees are often the easiest bridge between classic comfort and more expressive fruit-driven lots.</p>
</div>

---

## None of This Is a Ranking

<div class="callout">
  <p class="callout-label">The actual takeaway</p>
  <p class="callout-body">There is no best processing method. There is only what kind of cup you feel like drinking.</p>
</div>

There's no best processing method. There's just what you feel like drinking.

Some mornings call for something clean and steady — coffee that shows up and does its job without asking for anything in return. Other mornings you want something bright and a little surprising. Processing is one of the biggest reasons those cups taste the way they do.

The other thing worth knowing: all of this happens because a lot of people, in a lot of places, make careful decisions about a tiny fruit so you can wake up and start the day on slightly better terms than you might otherwise.

That feels worth paying attention to.
